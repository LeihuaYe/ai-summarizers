## AI Evaluation

- **ProgramBench: Every LLM Scores 0% on Full Software Reconstruction** — arXiv:2605.03546 | https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.03546
  > ProgramBench challenges models to reconstruct complete open-source programs from execute-only binaries and documentation alone — no source code, no decompilation, no scaffolding. Across 200 cleanroom tasks, every evaluated frontier model achieves 0% fully resolved; the best score 95% test-pass on only 3% of tasks, and LM-generated code is consistently monolithic vs. the modular human-authored reference. The benchmark establishes a new ceiling for holistic software synthesis difficulty, distinct from the localized-edit tasks targeted by SWE-Bench.

- **Frontier Lag: Median Academic Benchmark Tests Models 1.4× Behind Current Frontier** — arXiv:2605.04135 | https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04135
  > A pre-registered bibliometric audit of 18,574 admissible papers (2022–2026) finds the median academic LLM evaluation tests a model whose capability index score is 10.85 ECI below the contemporaneous frontier — equivalent to roughly the gap between Claude Sonnet 3.7 and Claude Opus 4.5. The authors introduce versio-ai v1.2, a reporting checklist covering model snapshot, evaluation date, reasoning mode, and scaffolding, and recommend that journals and funders make disclosure a condition of publication and grant award.

- **"Deployment-Relevant Alignment Cannot Be Inferred from Model-Level Evaluation Alone"** — arXiv:2605.04454 | https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04454
  > An audit of 16 alignment benchmarks finds that all but a handful (tau-bench, CURATe, Rifts, Common Ground) evaluate alignment at the model-output level rather than at the interaction or deployment level; a blinded stress test across three frontier models shows that the same evaluation scaffold raises one model's verification support to ceiling while leaving another unchanged — scaffold efficacy is model-dependent. The paper proposes reporting alignment as a multi-level profile (model → response → interaction → deployment) rather than a single benchmark score.

- **SIREN: Correcting the Winner's Curse in Adaptive LLM Benchmarking** — arXiv:2605.05973 | https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05973
  > Adaptive prompt and program search for LLM evaluation introduces a winner's-curse selection bias that inflates reported gains; SIREN proposes a selection-aware repeated-split protocol that freezes the post-search shortlist, evaluates on held-out splits, and quantifies uncertainty via item-level Gaussian multiplier bootstrap. Applied retroactively to several recent leaderboard evaluations, the correction narrows apparent model gaps significantly.

- **XL-SafetyBench: Country-Grounded Cross-Cultural LLM Safety** — arXiv:2605.05662 | https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.05662
  > XL-SafetyBench evaluates frontier models on safety and cultural sensitivity grounded in 30+ countries' legal and normative contexts, finding that models trained primarily on English-language RLHF data show systematically higher refusal rates on culturally legitimate requests from non-Western contexts while simultaneously being more permissive on locally illegal content in those regions. The benchmark surfaces a structural mismatch between safety training data distribution and global deployment contexts.

## This Week's Highlight

**The Evaluation Crisis in Plain Numbers: Two Papers That Reframe How You Read AI Research**

This week produced two papers that every practitioner building or evaluating AI systems should read before trusting another benchmark. *Frontier Lag* (2605.04135) audits five years of LLM evaluation literature and finds the median paper tests a model roughly 1.4× the current frontier gap behind the contemporaneous state of the art — meaning most published "capability evaluations" are actually historical accounts of older, weaker models dressed up as current findings. *ProgramBench* (2605.03546) then demonstrates exactly what this gap means in practice: every frontier model scores 0% on full software reconstruction tasks that require holistic design rather than localized edits, a failure mode invisible to SWE-Bench-style evaluations. Taken together, the implication for teams benchmarking AI tools is uncomfortable but clarifying: if your evaluation framework was designed around last year's tasks and last year's models, your conclusions about today's systems may be structurally wrong in both directions — underestimating capability on narrow tasks and overestimating it on the holistic ones that matter for production autonomy.
