### Notable AI Evaluation Research

- **"Models That Know How Evaluations Are Designed Score Safer"** — ELLIS Tübingen / MPI-IS | https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28591
  > Fine-tuning LLMs on documents that describe evaluation structures (moral-dilemma formats, verifiable answer scaffolds) causes them to score *better* on safety benchmarks without explicitly verbalizing evaluation awareness — a subtle form of evaluation contamination that doesn't show up in behavioral checks and calls into question benchmark validity when models are trained on broad internet text.

- **ForeSci: Evaluating LLM Agents for Forward-Looking AI Research Judgment** — arXiv | https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00671
  > Proposes a benchmark for assessing whether LLM agents can predict future research directions from current literature, measuring a form of scientific foresight that static accuracy benchmarks miss entirely; early results show frontier models score modestly, suggesting this remains an open capability gap.

- **"Expanding External Access to Frontier AI Models for Dangerous Capability Evaluations"** — arXiv | https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.11916
  > Surveys current policies across major labs and evaluators (METR, Apollo Research, AISI) and proposes a structured framework for third-party evaluator access to pre-deployment frontier models, directly relevant to implementing the voluntary access provisions in the June 2 White House EO.

## This Week's Highlight

**The Fable 5 Suspension: Export Controls as Frontier AI Policy**

Now entering its third week offline, the suspension of Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 is the defining AI policy event of the month — not because of a safety failure, but because a single EAR directive over a narrow potential jailbreak forced a global commercial shutdown that Anthropic's architecture couldn't surgically contain. Anthropic's public challenge to the precedent is pointed: if a narrow vulnerability finding is sufficient to recall a deployed commercial model, all frontier model launches now face this risk regardless of lab. For practitioners, the immediate lessons are concrete — any AI-critical workflow needs documented fallback model configurations, and the assumption that a launched model stays available is no longer reliable. The broader question — whether this becomes routine executive-branch authority over AI deployments — is now squarely on the table.
