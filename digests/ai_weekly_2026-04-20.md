## 2. New Benchmarks & Evaluation Frameworks

- **LM Council — Independent Third-Party Leaderboard** — Center for AI Safety / LM Council | [lmcouncil.ai](https://lmcouncil.ai/benchmarks)
  > Created in partnership with the Center for AI Safety, LM Council evaluates GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and others on a shared 2,500-question multimodal set (mathematics, humanities, natural sciences) contributed by ~1,000 domain experts. Unlike lab-self-reported benchmarks, scores are verified by a neutral third party.

- **Humanity's Last Exam (HLE) — Published in Nature** — Nature | [kili-technology.com](https://kili-technology.com/blog/ai-benchmarks-guide-the-top-evaluations-in-2026-and-why-theyre-not-enough)
  > 2,500 expert-crafted questions targeting the boundaries of known knowledge across dozens of academic fields. Now the de-facto ceiling benchmark for closed-ended LLM evaluation; leading frontier models (Claude Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro) are crossing 50% for the first time, per Stanford's AI Index 2026.

- **AEMA — Process-Aware Multi-Agent Evaluation Framework** — arXiv | [devflokers.com](https://www.devflokers.com/blog/new-ai-papers-arxiv-last-24-hours-april-2026-model-releases-breakthroughs)
  > AEMA (Auditable and Evaluable Multi-Agent) presents a verifiable, process-aware framework for assessing agentic LLM pipelines with full audit trails — addressing reproducibility gaps in existing agent benchmarks.

- **AIware 2026 Benchmark & Dataset Track** — AIware 2026 | [2026.aiwareconf.org](https://2026.aiwareconf.org/track/aiware-2026-benchmark---dataset-track)
  > New conference track dedicated to rigorous benchmark and dataset contributions for AI in software engineering; submissions due in the coming weeks.

## 3. Notable AI Evaluation Research

- **"Audit Cards: Contextualizing AI Evaluations"** — arXiv:2504.13839 | [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.13839)
  > Proposes standardized *audit cards* to attach metadata (evaluation context, assumptions, limitations) to AI eval results — analogous to model cards but for evaluation protocols — addressing the problem of decontextualized benchmark numbers being misapplied.

- **"Towards a Science of AI Agent Reliability"** — arXiv | [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.16666v1)
  > Defines twelve concrete reliability metrics decomposed across four dimensions — consistency, robustness, predictability, and safety — offering a principled framework for evaluating agents beyond task success rates.

- **"The 2025 AI Agent Index"** — arXiv:2602.17753 | [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.17753v1)
  > Documents technical and safety features of deployed agentic AI systems across dozens of public deployments; highlights significant heterogeneity in safety disclosures and a lack of standardized capability reporting.

- **Stanford AI Index 2026 — Safety Benchmarks Falling Behind Capabilities** — Stanford HAI | [hai.stanford.edu](https://hai.stanford.edu/news/inside-the-ai-index-12-takeaways-from-the-2026-report) · [The Decoder](https://the-decoder.com/stanfords-ai-index-2026-shows-rapid-progress-growing-safety-concerns-and-declining-public-trust/)
  > The 2026 report documents SWE-bench Verified rising from ~60% to near 100% in a single year; the US–China capability gap has essentially closed. The Foundation Model Transparency Index average dropped from 58 to 40 — the most capable models are now among the least transparent. AI incidents rose to 362 (up from 233 in 2024). Responsible-AI benchmarks covering safety, fairness, and factuality are "largely absent" from frontier developers' reported results.

- **METR and Apollo Research — Ongoing Agentic Capability Evaluations** — METR / Apollo Research | [metr.org](https://metr.org/) · [apolloresearch.ai](https://www.apolloresearch.ai/)
  > Both orgs continue pre-deployment evaluations of frontier models. Apollo Research's focus this cycle is on scheming detection — eliciting and measuring deceptive goal-directed behavior in state-of-the-art models.

## This Week's Highlight

**Claude Opus 4.7 + Stanford's Transparency Warning.** The headline release of the week is Anthropic's Opus 4.7 — the first GA Claude model with high-resolution vision and long-horizon task budgets, now directly competing on agentic coding with near-SOTA SWE-bench numbers. But the Stanford AI Index provides essential context: as models become more capable, they are simultaneously becoming *less* transparent (Foundation Model Transparency Index dropped 18 points year-over-year) and AI incidents are surging. Practitioners should treat benchmark claims with greater skepticism, lean on third-party evaluators like LM Council and METR, and track California's CAIA closely as the first major US AI accountability law to take effect this cycle.
